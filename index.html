The Writings of a Warrior Poet

Combat veteran. Father. Poet. Writing the war after the war.

No Medal for This

No one pins you for choosing not to rage.
No parade for walking past the bottle.
No ribbons for watching your tone
when your chest feels like a minefield.

But this?
This is where the war is real.

Where I sit with pain like it’s intel,
not enemy. Where I let my daughter cry
and don’t flinch—because I finally know
tears aren’t weakness, they’re clearance for truth.

No one sees it, but that’s okay.
I don’t do it for them.
I do it so she never wonders
if her father chose silence over healing.

Because the hardest thing I ever learned
was how to stay after surviving.

The Things She’ll Never Know

She’ll never know
how many times I stood outside her door,
hand raised to knock—
not because she needed me,
but because I needed her laughter
to pull me back
from whatever dark I’d wandered into.

She’ll never know
the number of times I rehearsed
“I’m proud of you”
like a mission brief,
because love always felt easier to show than to say.

She’ll never know
that every night terror I fought alone
was followed by a whisper:
“You still have to wake up for her.”

The Weight

It’s not just the ruck I carried
or the rounds I fired.

It’s the names I never say aloud,
the photos I can’t delete,
the way I scan every exit—even in peace.

It’s waking up three times a night
and lying to the mirror by morning.
It’s knowing the war didn’t stay
where I left it.

And it’s not killing me.
It’s just making everything heavier.

Blink

You’re folding laundry
and suddenly it’s Kandahar again.
Same hands,
but they’re shaking now.

A car backfires
and your ribs pull tight
like they remember
what your brain forgot to warn you about.

It’s not flashbacks.
It’s worse.
It’s memory ambush in the middle of
normal.

And no one sees you flinch.
Not even when you blink
and it’s all back.
